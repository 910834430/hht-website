Abstract

A frequency reconfigurable patch antenna is designed and simulated. The proposed antenna consists of 2 slots together with three extra copper films. They are all connected with the main radiating patch by PIN diodes to achieve frequency reconfiguration. The antenna has one port excited with a microstrip line feed mechanism. A parametric study has been carried out to understand the impact of different parameters(patch size, slots length, and PIN diode switches position)on the antenna and optimize its performance. The overall dimension of the antenna is set at 47.99 x 45.37 x 1.67mm^3. For different PIN diode configurations, the antenna can operate at a double band mode:2.4GHz and 5.7GHz, three single-band modes 2.135GHz, 1.86GHz, 1.6GHz. All the simulated results are obtained from CST Studio Suite 2019.

1.       Introduction

The antenna is the backbone of wireless communication. How to make a powerful antenna with desirable functionality is essential. Many development and improvement have been made in the last two decades. One idea is to design a single antenna that can dynamically change its operating frequency or radiation pattern to support multiple telecommunications standards. An antenna that exhibits this kind of behaviour is referred to as a reconfigurable antenna as it can reconfigure its resonant frequency or polarization. Typically frequency reconfigurability is achieved using varactor diode, PIN diodes or tunable materials to adjust their frequency of operation[1]. This approach raised considerable attention for it enables the antenna to use power and spectrum more efficiently, which will result in a reduction of complicated hardware, meaning lower the cost of the system. This project is inspired and motivated by the same reason. A patch antenna is chosen as a template to develop because it is relatively easier to analyze. Slots and extra elements are added to make the patch exhibit reconfigurability. The dimensions are obtained by parametric study. There are four states that it can operate, which is controlled by PIN diodes configuration.

2.       Literature Review

The development of the antenna starts in 1888. German physicist Heinrich Hertz built the world first dipole antenna, which is intended to prove waves that electromagnetic theory suggests. Over the years, lots of different kind of antennas have been developed for different purposes. Yagi-Uda Antenna uses “parasitic elements”(an array of linear dipoles) to change the radiation pattern of the transmitted waves or their resonant frequency. Horn Antennas, which shaped like a horn, is particularly useful in high-frequency applications. However, they can operate a wide range of frequencies because they have no resonant elements. They are often used as feed antenna for larger antenna structures. Furthermore, there is the recognizable dish antenna(often known as a parabolic reflector) for satellite communications for its high gain and highly directional features. In 1970, the microstrip antenna received considerable attention. The merit of this antenna is that it is inexpensive, lightweight and easy to install. Because of these features, it is very beneficial for aircraft, spacecraft, missile applications. They are vital in modern communication systems.

The reconfigurable antenna appeared in 1930. Reconfigurable features are desirable for it makes one antenna capable of operating multiple systems. This approach lows the costs and improves the performance of the communication systems. Those kinds of the antenna can be derived from the Yagi antenna, array antenna or patch antenna. They can vary their operating frequency, radiation pattern or polarization. The modern reconfigurable antenna is very sophisticated. In[2], a 3D Reconfigurable Frequency Selective Surface antenna is studied. The FSS unit-cell is designed to control the transmission characteristics of EM waves by controlling switching devices. A frequency-reconfigurable Bow-Tie antenna introduced in [3]. By embedding PIN diodes over the bow-tie arms, the effective electrical length of the antenna can be altered, making an electrically tunable operating band. There is also an antenna array with the flexibility of switching multiple antenna properties. A switchable-frequency slot-ring antenna element for designing a reconfigurable array shown in [4], the aperture of an L band slot-ring antenna can be reconfigured to a two × 2 C-band slot-ring antenna array by changing the states of 16 PIN diodes.

Most of the above design approaches invoke PIN diode or transistor to achieve reconfigurability. Those "switches" are typically attached to the antennas through soldering or epoxies, leading to reliability problems. In addition, incorporate those switches into antennas are expensive as they often require complicated and costly fabrication processes. However, in [5], Pattern Reconfigurable Antenna Based on Monolithically Printed VO2 Switch is designed. The antenna's electrical length is altered by turning on and off the custom-designed vanadium dioxide (VO2) ink-based monolithically printed switch. Thus operating frequency band can be shifted.

This paper presents a microstrip patch antenna driven by the facts that it is intuitive to understand, cheap to manufacture, easy to implement reconfiguring features. The following paper has been studied to appreciate their design insight. In [6], the rectangular patch antenna has additional radiation elements connected to the main patch by PIN diodes to achieve frequency reconfigurability. The triangular cut in the upper right-hand corner makes the antenna tunable for the pattern as well. In[7], the antenna is cut in the main patch forming two slots. They are incorporated to perturb the surface current path, introducing the local inductive effect responsible for the excitation of the second resonant mode. While in[8], Yagi Uda shaped patch antenna is designed by connecting the patch with extra copper film using PIN diodes. Essentially the length of the patch is altered. Thus frequency band can be shifted accordingly.

3.       Methods

3.1.        Background study

Wireless communication is mainly achieved by transmitting or receiving electromagnetic waves (radio waves)where information is embedded. For any electric signal, for example, a sinusoidal voltage signal. How to propagating this signal and propagate it effectively is essential. Consider a dipole antenna shown in Figure 1. Alternating voltage source Vi is constantly moving charges at the end of the rod, making it from positively charged to negatively charged. Oscillating charges are created as a result(Figure 2). As the charges approach or depart from each other, the varying electric field automatically generates a varying magnetic field perpendicular to it. Thus wave propagation is achieved. In this case frequency of the antenna is equal to the frequency of the input voltage signal. For a perfect transmission, the length of the antenna should be half of the wavelength.

     

Figure 1. A dipole antenna [9].         Figure 2. A pair of oscillating charges and their electric filed [10].

Now consider a more advanced antenna - microstrip patch antenna shown in Figure 3. It consisted of a thick substrate material sitting on the ground plane. The patch is mounted on the top of the substrate. When the port is excited with a signal, the current is at a peak at the centre of the patch. Moreover, there is no current at the edges because the current has no exits. Therefore the voltage is 90 degree out of phase with the current. And it peaks at the edges and has the lowest magnitude at the centre. The electrical field is presented in Figure 4 in the form of arrows. The vertical component of those vectors does not produce radiation that can be propagated as they cancel each other, and the copper layers trapped them inside. Curved arrows represent the fringing effect(the electric field line will not be vertical because it is in the edges ). These fringing fields have a horizontal component that will add up and be responsible for the radiation.

       

Figure 3. Patch antenna structure [11].          Figure 4. Electric field of operating patch antenna [12].

5.       Results and discussion

5.1.        Dual band mode

 

                   

Figure 22. 2.4GHz and 5.8GHz radiation pattern - dual band mode.                              

Figure 22 shows the radiation pattern obtained from simulation results when the diodes are in OFF condition. The performance of the reconfigurable antenna is good for multi-band frequency at 2.4 GHz and 5.8 GHz. The main lobe magnitude at 2.4 GHz is 5.41 dB, while at 5.8 GHz, the value is 6.31 dB. Besides that, angular width (3 dB) is 101.9˚ (2.4 GHz) and 60.6˚(5.8 GHz). The radiation direction is not perfectly horizontal for the elevation plane, but it is not deviating much(5˚and 10˚). This effect can be removed by adjusting the antenna position correspondingly. Side lobes are small compared to the main lobes with a magnitude of -2dBi and -5dBi. Angular width is acceptable for it is not overly wide. Main lobe gains are sufficient enough for communication. In this mode, the performance of the antenna is satisfactory.

5.2.        D1 D2 D3

     

Figure 23. Radiation patterns when diode is configured at D1 D2 D3 respectively.

Figure 23 shows the radiation pattern obtained from simulation results when the PIN diodes are switched on, connecting the patch to the extra copper films in terms. The main lobe magnitudes are  5.28dB, 4.76dB and 3.97dB respectively. Also, angular width (3 dB) are 100˚ (2.1 GHz),105.7 ˚(1.8 GHz) and 110 ˚(1.6GHz). The performance of the antenna degrades each time moving from D1 to D3. The antenna is weaker in power when there are more additional elements. Moreover, the angular width is wide, so there would be a lot of wasted energy. The side lobe looks horrible when it comes to D3 state with a magnitude of 2dBi. To sum up, the result showed in D2 and D3 state is not satisfactory.

There could be lots of reason causing a weak gain or strong side lobes. The Lumped elements used to model the PIN diode was set at 4.6 Ohm in series with a 2.6e-13 Henry inductor. In D2 or D3, the antenna is resonating at 1.8GHz and 1.6GHz, respectively. The overall impedance of modelled PIN diode is around 3 Ohm in those modes. In our design, each additional copper films are connected by three diodes. As the current travel from bottom to top, the more diode it is passing, the weaker it becomes. Figure 24 and Figure 25 show there is only a few currents in the surface that can make it to the top. It will result in a decrease in the magnitude of antenna gain. A way around this issue is to use more diodes in parallel, lowing the overall impedance and allowing more current to pass simultaneously. However, lots of diodes soldered in parallel means the fabrication process will be more complex. An increase in cost will be inevitable. A more reasonable approach will be to invest in other switches(other transistors) and simulated the performances.

Side lobes are unwanted in any case, for it means there is wasted energy when transmitting signals. It also means that when an antenna works as the receiver, it will pick up unwanted signals from directions that side lobes suggest. It will also become a noise source to the nearby receiver antenna. Side lobes in D2 and D3 are large in magnitude. Side lobes often come from the antennas structures themselves. There are two known places for our patch that accounted for radiation: the front end and back end of the patch, as mentioned in the background study. However, for state D1 D2 D3, an extra film can be realized as a small patch. So there will be radiation at the front end also at the back end, which can be built up and responsible for the side lobes. Figure 24 and Figure 25 showed some current in the surface of the extra copper films going backwards or in other directions, which contributes to side lobes. A physical block can be built as a shield around the patch to block the unwanted radiations to fix this problem. However, this approach will make the antenna less compact and add in more weight. EM wave blocks typically are made of steels which is very heavy. Given a fixed antenna structure, there is not much can do as there is always be radiation at the back end of the copper film. Modify the shape of the extra copper films to (triangular, circular or other customized shape)may improve the side lobes. Investigation can be made around that idea.

6.       Conclusion

In this paper, a frequency reconfigurable patch microstrip patch antenna for multi-mode mobile communication is designed and simulated. The slots alter surface current distribution, achieving frequency reconfiguration. Additional copper film changes the antenna's electrical length, making it work at different frequency bands. The antenna works at five different bands: 2.4GHz(Wi-Fi and Bluetooth), 5.8GHz(WLAN), 2.1GHz(UMTS), 1.8GHz(GSM1800) and 1.6GHz(GPS). After simulation, band around 1.8GHz and 1.6GHz may not work due to weak gain and large side lobes. Using more diode and construct a physical block may fix those issues.
